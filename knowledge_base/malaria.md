# Malaria

## What is Malaria?
Malaria is a serious, sometimes life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites. It's transmitted through the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. Malaria is common in tropical and subtropical regions including parts of Africa, Asia, and South America.

## What Does Malaria Feel Like?
Symptoms usually appear 10-15 days after mosquito bite:

### Classic Malaria Symptoms:
- **High fever** (comes in cycles)
- **Chills and shivering** (feeling extremely cold)
- **Heavy sweating** (as fever breaks)
- **Headache** (often severe)
- **Body aches** and muscle pain
- **Nausea and vomiting**
- **Diarrhea** (sometimes)
- **Fatigue** and weakness
- **Loss of appetite**

### The Malaria Cycle:
Malaria often follows a pattern that repeats every 24-72 hours:
1. **Cold stage**: Intense shivering and chills (1-2 hours)
2. **Hot stage**: High fever, headache, vomiting (2-6 hours)
3. **Sweating stage**: Heavy sweating, temperature drops, exhaustion (2-4 hours)

## Types of Malaria:
- **Plasmodium falciparum**: Most dangerous, can be fatal
- **Plasmodium vivax**: Can remain dormant in liver and reactivate months later
- **Plasmodium ovale**: Similar to vivax
- **Plasmodium malariae**: Milder but can last years
- **Plasmodium knowlesi**: Found in Southeast Asia

## Severe Malaria - Medical Emergency:
Go to hospital immediately if you experience:
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Seizures
- Severe anemia (pale skin, extreme weakness)
- Difficulty breathing
- Abnormal bleeding
- Jaundice (yellow skin and eyes)
- Dark or bloody urine
- Organ failure

Severe malaria can be fatal within 24 hours without treatment.

## How Do You Get Malaria?
- Bite from infected Anopheles mosquito
- Mosquitoes are most active between dusk and dawn
- NOT contagious from person to person
- Can also spread through blood transfusion or organ transplant (rare)

## What to Do If You Suspect Malaria:

### See a Doctor IMMEDIATELY:
- Get a blood test to confirm malaria
- Early treatment is critical
- Tell doctor if you've traveled to malaria-prone areas

### Treatment:
Malaria is treatable with prescription medications:
- **Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT)**: Most common treatment
- **Chloroquine**: Used in some regions
- **Quinine**: For severe cases
- **Primaquine**: For vivax/ovale malaria (kills dormant liver parasites)

**Important**: Complete the full course of medication even if you feel better.

### Hospital Treatment for Severe Cases:
- Intravenous (IV) medications
- Oxygen therapy
- Blood transfusion if severely anemic
- Intensive care monitoring

## How Long Does Treatment Take?
- **Uncomplicated malaria**: 3-7 days of medication
- **Severe malaria**: Hospitalization for several days
- **Full recovery**: 2-6 weeks
- You should start feeling better within 24-48 hours of starting treatment

## Prevention:

### If Traveling to Malaria Areas:
- Take antimalarial medication before, during, and after travel
- Start medication 1-2 weeks before travel (as prescribed)
- Continue for 4 weeks after returning

### Avoid Mosquito Bites:
- Sleep under insecticide-treated mosquito nets
- Wear long sleeves and pants (especially evening to dawn)
- Use DEET-based mosquito repellent
- Use indoor insect sprays
- Stay in air-conditioned or well-screened rooms
- Wear permethrin-treated clothing

### Reduce Mosquito Breeding:
- Eliminate standing water around your home
- Support community mosquito control programs

## No Vaccine Widely Available:
While some malaria vaccines are being developed and used in limited areas, prevention through bite avoidance and preventive medications remains the primary strategy.

## Can You Get Malaria More Than Once?
Yes, you can get malaria multiple times. Previous infection provides partial immunity but not complete protection.

## Special Considerations:

### Pregnant Women:
- Malaria is more severe during pregnancy
- Can cause miscarriage, stillbirth, or low birth weight
- Requires immediate medical attention
- Some antimalarial drugs are not safe during pregnancy

### Children:
- Children under 5 are at highest risk
- Symptoms can progress rapidly
- May not show classic fever cycle
- Require immediate medical care

### Travelers:
- Always take preventive medication
- Seek medical help immediately if symptoms develop
- Tell healthcare provider about your travel history

## Diagnosis:
- Blood smear test (microscope)
- Rapid diagnostic test (RDT)
- PCR test (more sensitive)

## Important Notes:
- Malaria symptoms can be similar to flu - get tested if you've been in malaria areas
- Don't self-medicate - proper diagnosis is essential
- Complete full treatment course
- Follow up with doctor to ensure parasites are cleared
- Vivax and ovale malaria can reactivate months later - may need additional treatment

## After Recovery:
- Rest adequately
- Eat nutritious food to rebuild strength
- Follow up blood tests as recommended
- Take steps to prevent reinfection
- Monitor for relapse (especially with vivax/ovale)

## When to Seek Emergency Care:
Any of these symptoms require immediate hospitalization:
- Confusion or unconsciousness
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe weakness (unable to stand or sit)
- Repeated vomiting (can't keep medications down)
- Seizures
- Yellow eyes or skin
- Very little urine output

Malaria is serious but treatable. The key is early diagnosis and proper medical care.
